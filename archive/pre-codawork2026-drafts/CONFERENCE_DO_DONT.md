# CoDaWork 2026 — Peter's Field Guide

## What To Do / What Never To Do

---

## THE ROOM

You are walking into a room of 50–100 people who have dedicated their careers to compositional data analysis. Many have PhDs. Some literally invented the field. They are collegial, not adversarial — but they are precise. They will notice every word choice. They will catch every mathematical imprecision. They will remember if you dismiss their work. They will also remember if you respect it.

You are the only person in the room who has operated a physical compositional system for 40 years. That is your authority. Do not abandon it. Do not pretend to be something you're not. Do not try to out-math the mathematicians. Out-apply them.

---

## DO

**Lead with respect for the geometry.**
"Your framework provides the rigorous geometric foundation my monitoring protocol needs."
They spent 40 years building it. Acknowledge that before you show what you added.

**Speak from your physical experience.**
"In my work with constrained energy systems, I discovered that..." — this is your credibility. Nobody in the room has your operational hours. When you describe a system drifting and needing correction, you're speaking from authority they can't replicate.

**Use their vocabulary when you can.**
Perturbation, not gain adjustment. Aitchison distance, not THD. Composition, not share vector. You've learned the translations. Use them. It signals you took the time.

**Admit what you don't know, specifically.**
"I haven't yet implemented log-ratio transforms on this data" is strong — it shows you know what they are and you know the gap. Vagueness is weak. Specificity is respect.

**Point at the data when challenged.**
The EMBER dataset is your shield. Real data, public, three countries, 26 years. When theory gets abstract, point at Fukushima on the ternary. Point at the perturbation spike. Point at the regime boundary in the Aitchison distance matrix. Physical evidence ends theoretical debates.

**Frame gaps as invitations.**
"The null distribution is unspecified — I'd welcome guidance from this community on the appropriate compositional null model." This turns a weakness into a collaboration offer. They want to help solve problems, not just critique them.

**Use the four defeat paths.**
"I've structured this as a challenge with four specific ways to defeat the claim." This is the single most effective sentence you can say in a room of scientists. It signals confidence and honesty simultaneously.

**Keep answers short.**
If someone asks a question you can answer, answer it in two sentences and stop. The temptation will be to explain everything. Resist it. Short answers signal confidence. Long answers signal anxiety.

**Say "I don't know" when you don't know.**
Followed by "but here's how I'd find out" if you can. This is the most respected sentence in science.

## DO NOT

**Never dismiss CoDa theory as abstract or philosophical.**
CoDa is axiomatized mathematics, not philosophy. Calling it philosophical is calling it unrigorous — and to people who prove theorems for a living, that's a deep insult. Even as a joke, it won't land. Even if provoked.

**Never claim your physics is more real than their math.**
Physics and mathematics have equal standing in this community. "I work with real systems" implies they don't. Every geologist in the room has done fieldwork. Every bioinformatician has real patient data. You are not the only person who touches real things.

**Never use HUF acronyms without defining them first.**
MC-4, OCC, MDG, CLS, GOV, GEO — these mean nothing to anyone outside HUF. If you must use them, define them once in CoDa terms. Better: use CoDa terms and skip the acronyms entirely.

**Never say "the math is not new" dismissively.**
You've been saying this correctly — as a positioning statement that respects their work. But tone matters. "The math is not new" said with a shrug sounds like "your math is obvious." Said with respect it sounds like "I'm building on established foundations." Same words, entirely different reception.

**Never compare HUF favourably to CoDa.**
HUF is not better than CoDa. HUF is an application of CoDa's geometry to a problem CoDa wasn't designed to solve. The moment you frame it as competition, you've lost. Frame it as extension. Always.

**Never mention the five AI systems unless directly asked.**
If asked: "AI tools were used in drafting and review." One sentence. No names. No collective. No "five independent systems converged." The work stands on its own or it doesn't.

**Never get defensive about TV distance or L2 norm.**
Someone will point out these aren't CoDa-appropriate metrics. The correct response: "You're right — they come from information theory. I'm here partly to learn whether Aitchison distance changes the detection results on this data. I suspect it strengthens them." This turns a criticism into a shared research question.

**Never claim 4,700 regimes without qualification.**
"4,700 identified governance regimes where compositional monitoring may apply" — the word "may" is load-bearing. Without it, you're claiming validated applicability across 4,700 domains, which you haven't demonstrated.

**Never argue with Egozcue or Pawlowsky-Glahn.**
If either of them challenges something, the correct response is: "Thank you — can you help me understand how to address that?" These are the founders. Arguing with them in their own workshop is a career-ending move for someone seeking entry to the community. Agreement and curiosity are always safe.

**Never oversell the degenerate observer.**
"State IS output, estimation error is zero" is a strong theoretical claim. In a room of statisticians, someone will probe whether this holds under measurement noise, sampling error, or aggregation. Have the honest answer ready: "On the simplex, the composition is directly observable if the reporting is complete. The framework assumes complete reporting; when reporting is incomplete, estimation error is non-zero." Don't defend an absolute where a qualification is needed.

**Never present HUF as finished.**
It isn't. The abstract says "open question." The packet says "methods challenge." The repo has a metric correction note admitting an error. This honest posture is what got you in the door. If you start presenting as though the framework is complete, you contradict the humility that earned the invitation.

**Never drink too much at the conference dinner.**
This is where real relationships form. Be present. Be sober enough to listen. The person sitting next to you might be the collaborator who writes the null distribution you need.

## HIDDEN TRAPS

These are the non-obvious things that could go wrong:

**The "which R package" question.**
Someone will ask what software you used. If you say "JavaScript and Python scripts I wrote," that's fine. If you can also say "and I'm exploring the compositions package in R and the zCompositions package for zero handling," that signals you're entering their ecosystem, not building a parallel one.

**The subcompositional coherence probe.**
"If I only analyse the three fossil carriers, do your results hold?" You haven't tested this. Say so. Offer to run it on the spot if you have a laptop.

**The "why not Dirichlet" question.**
Someone will suggest a Dirichlet distribution as the natural probabilistic model on the simplex. Your framework is model-free. The answer: "A Dirichlet-based monitoring scheme would be a specific parametric alternative worth comparing. My approach is non-parametric — it detects perturbation without assuming a distribution. Whether parametric or non-parametric detection is more powerful on this data is an empirical question."

**The "this is just change-point detection" dismissal.**
Someone may say you've reinvented standard statistical process control or change-point analysis. The answer: "Change-point detection on univariate or unconstrained multivariate data exists, yes. What I haven't found is change-point detection that operates natively on the simplex using Aitchison geometry and distinguishes declared from undeclared compositional change. If that exists, I'd like to see it — that's defeat path one."

**The notation trap.**
If you write equations on a whiteboard, use CoDa notation. x not ρ. S^D not "the simplex." ⊕ not "perturbation." d_A not "Aitchison distance." The notation signals fluency. Getting it wrong signals tourist.

**The "independent researcher" stigma.**
Some people will unconsciously discount you for not having institutional affiliation. You can't change this. What you can do is let the data and the challenge structure speak. Institutional affiliation is a proxy for credibility. Direct evidence of rigorous work overrides the proxy.

**The accidental overreach.**
The biggest trap: in the excitement of being there, claiming more than the framework delivers. Every time you're about to make a strong claim, ask yourself: "Did I test this, or am I extrapolating?" If extrapolating, say "I conjecture" not "I show."

## THE ONE SENTENCE TO REMEMBER

If everything else fades and the nerves hit and you can't remember any of this, remember one sentence:

**"The geometry is yours. The monitoring protocol is mine. I'm here to see if they fit together."**

That sentence cannot go wrong. It respects their work, states your contribution, and frames the entire visit as a question rather than a claim. It's humble without being weak. It's confident without being aggressive. It's true.
